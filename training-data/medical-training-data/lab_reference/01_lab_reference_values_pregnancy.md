## ⚠️ IMPORTANT NOTE ON REFERENCE RANGES
Pregnancy significantly alters many laboratory values. Standard non-pregnancy reference ranges should NOT be used during pregnancy, as they may:
- Lead to over-diagnosis (flagging normal pregnancy changes as pathological)
- Mask true pathology (normalize truly abnormal values)
Always use pregnancy-specific or trimester-specific reference intervals from the laboratory that performed the test whenever available.

## HEMATOLOGY (CBC) CHANGES IN PREGNANCY

### Plasma Volume & Blood Volume
- Plasma volume begins increasing at ~6 weeks gestation
- By 26 weeks: >50% above pre-pregnancy levels
- Plateaus for remainder of pregnancy
- Red cell mass also increases, but LESS than plasma volume
- Net result: **dilutional (physiological) anemia**

### Hemoglobin (Hb)
- Declines due to hemodilution; lowest point typically in second trimester, then rises slightly in third
- WHO Anemia Thresholds in Pregnancy:
  - First trimester: **Hb <110 g/L (11.0 g/dL)**
  - Second trimester: **Hb <105 g/L (10.5 g/dL)**
  - Third trimester: **Hb <110 g/L (11.0 g/dL)**
- Anemia affects >40% of pregnant women worldwide
- Highest prevalence: South-East Asia (48.7%), Africa (46.3%)

### Hematocrit (Hct)
- Falls proportionally with hemoglobin

### White Blood Cell Count (WBC)
- Increases significantly during pregnancy, especially second trimester
- Leukocytosis (WBC up to 15–16 × 10⁹/L) is NORMAL in pregnancy
- WBC can further increase in labor and postpartum (up to 25–30 × 10⁹/L in labor)
- Cause: mainly neutrophilia (increased neutrophil production)

### Platelet Count (PLT)
- Gradually decreases throughout pregnancy
- Gestational thrombocytopenia: PLT <150 × 10⁹/L in pregnancy (benign, physiological)
  - Affects: 3% (T1), 12% (T2), 23% (T3) in some studies
  - Resolves spontaneously after delivery
  - Requires no treatment
  - MUST distinguish from: immune thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP), HELLP syndrome, TTP
- True pathological thrombocytopenia: PLT <100 × 10⁹/L (HELLP cutoff: <100; severe <50)

### Red Cell Indices
- MCV: relatively stable; may slightly increase (macrocytosis from folate demands)
- MCH, MCHC: relatively stable; decreased in iron deficiency
- RDW: may increase (reflects mixed cell population in iron/folate deficiency)

### Reticulocyte Count
- May increase slightly (bone marrow activity increases)
- Reticulocytes >3% in anemia → hemolysis or blood loss
- Reticulocytes <3% in anemia → hypoproliferative (iron/B12/folate deficiency)

### Folate and B12
- Serum total B12 falls significantly in pregnancy (reduction in holohaptocorrin)
- Holotranscobalamin (active B12) does NOT change — use this for B12 status assessment
- Red cell folate levels decrease in women not taking supplements
- Recommended: folic acid 400–800 mcg/day preconception and first trimester (neural tube defect prevention)

## BIOCHEMISTRY / CHEMISTRY CHANGES IN PREGNANCY

### Liver Function Tests

| Parameter | Change in Pregnancy | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| ALT (alanine aminotransferase) | Mild decrease | Lower than non-pregnant baseline |
| AST (aspartate aminotransferase) | Mild decrease | Lower than non-pregnant baseline |
| GGT (gamma-glutamyl transferase) | Mild decrease | |
| Bilirubin (total + unconjugated) | Mild reduction | |
| ALP (alkaline phosphatase) | **Significantly INCREASED** | Due to placental isoenzyme; cannot use as liver disease marker in pregnancy |
| Albumin | Decreased | Despite 50% increase in synthesis; possible amino acid catabolism for fetus |
| LDH | Variable; may increase (up to 2x pre-pregnancy in third trimester) | |

**Important:**
- Elevated ALP in pregnancy = NORMAL (placental ALP)
- Distinguish placental ALP from liver/bone by electrophoresis if needed
- Acute rise in ALP may signify placental damage or infarction
- ALT/AST ELEVATION in pregnancy is ABNORMAL → evaluate for HELLP, ICP, AFLP, hepatitis, PG
- LDH:AST ratio >22: may distinguish TTP from HELLP syndrome
- Raised serum bile acids = best indicator of ICP (transaminases elevated in ~50% of ICP cases; GGT in ~1/3; bilirubin mildly increased in ~30%)

### Renal Function

| Parameter | Change in Pregnancy |
|---|---|
| Serum creatinine | **DECREASED** (increased GFR dilutes creatinine) |
| BUN (blood urea nitrogen) | Decreased |
| Uric acid (serum urate) | Decreased in first/second trimester; may increase in third trimester |
| eGFR | INCREASES — formulae not validated in pregnancy; do not use |
| Proteinuria | Increases (especially from 20 weeks) — up to 300 mg/24h is normal |
| GFR | Increases ~50% above baseline by second trimester |

**Important:**
- Normal creatinine in pregnancy = lower than non-pregnant normal
- Creatinine "normal" at 0.9–1.0 mg/dL in pregnancy = concerning (true elevation for pregnancy)
- Proteinuria >300 mg/24h or protein:creatinine >0.3 → preeclampsia threshold
- Creatinine rises from 2nd to 3rd trimester especially in pre-existing renal disease
- Acute kidney injury risk: HELLP (60–80% of AKI in pregnancy from PET/HELLP)

### Electrolytes

| Electrolyte | Change |
|---|---|
| Sodium | Slightly decreased (dilutional) |
| Potassium | Relatively stable; hyperkalemia rare but reported with labetalol, Mg infusion |
| Bicarbonate | Slightly decreased (respiratory alkalosis compensation) |
| Chloride | Relatively stable |
| Calcium | Total calcium decreased; ionized calcium relatively stable |
| Magnesium | Slightly decreased |
| Phosphate | Relatively stable |

**Hyponatremia in pregnancy:**
- SIADH picture: prolonged labor, post-CS, NSAID use
- Severe hyponatremia: oxytocin infusion (antidiuretic effect), hyperemesis gravidarum, primary polydipsia
- Peripartum hyponatremia: exclude Sheehan syndrome and lymphocytic hypophysitis

**Gestational diabetes insipidus:**
- Rare; 2nd/3rd trimester
- Excess placental vasopressinase activity
- Also seen in AFLP, HELLP with hepatic injury

### Thyroid Function (see dedicated thyroid file)
| Parameter | Change in Pregnancy |
|---|---|
| TSH | Suppressed in first trimester (hCG effect); use trimester-specific ranges |
| Free T4 (FT4) | Slight increase in first trimester; then stabilizes |
| Total T3, Total T4 | Significantly INCREASED (increased TBG) — use FREE forms for assessment |
| TBG (Thyroid Binding Globulin) | Significantly increased |

### Glucose
- Fasting glucose: lower in pregnancy (placental glucose consumption)
- GDM screening at 24–28 weeks using OGTT (see GDM file)
- Normal fasting glucose in pregnancy: ~65–75 mg/dL (lower than non-pregnant)

### Hormones

| Hormone | Pregnancy Values |
|---|---|
| β-hCG | Doubles every 2 days in early viable IUP; peaks 8–12 weeks |
| Progesterone | T1: 725–4400 ng/dL; T2: 1950–8250 ng/dL; T3: 6500–22,900 ng/dL |
| Prolactin | 20–400 ng/mL (vs. 3–27 in non-pregnant females) |
| Estradiol | Rises dramatically throughout pregnancy |
| hPL (human placental lactogen) | Rises with gestational age |
| AFP (alpha-fetoprotein) | Normal adult <40 ng/mL; elevated in NTDs, elevated with certain fetal conditions |

### Coagulation
- Pregnancy is a **procoagulant state** (preparation for delivery hemostasis)
- Increased: fibrinogen, factors VII, VIII, X, XII, vWF
- Decreased: Protein S; Protein C relatively stable; ATIII relatively stable
- PT, aPTT: slightly shortened or unchanged
- D-dimer: elevated in normal pregnancy (cannot use standard cutoffs)
- Fibrinogen: significantly elevated (normal in pregnancy: 350–650 mg/dL vs. 200–400 in non-pregnant)

**DIC markers in HELLP:**
- Low fibrinogen (<200 mg/dL in setting of HELLP = severe DIC)
- Elevated D-dimer
- Schistocytes on blood smear

### Iron Studies
- Serum ferritin: most reliable marker of iron stores (unaffected by hemodilution)
- Ferritin <12–30 ng/mL → iron deficiency even with normal Hb
- Serum iron: decreases; TIBC: increases (as in non-pregnant iron deficiency)
- Hemoglobin does not fall until iron stores fully depleted

## β-hCG REFERENCE RANGES IN PREGNANCY
| Gestational Age | Expected β-hCG Range (mIU/mL) |
|---|---|
| 3 weeks | 5–50 |
| 4 weeks | 5–426 |
| 5 weeks | 18–7,340 |
| 6 weeks | 1,080–56,500 |
| 7–8 weeks | 7,650–229,000 |
| 9–12 weeks | 25,700–288,000 (peak) |
| 13–16 weeks | 13,300–254,000 |
| 17–24 weeks | 4,060–165,400 |
| 25–40 weeks | 3,640–117,000 |

Key points:
- β-hCG >2,000 mIU/mL without visible IUP on TVUS = high concern for ectopic pregnancy
- β-hCG should double every ~48 hours in viable IUP (when <1500 mIU/mL)
- Declining β-hCG (>21% decrease/48h) = failing pregnancy or resolving ectopic
