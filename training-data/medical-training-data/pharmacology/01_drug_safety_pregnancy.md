## TERATOGENICITY — KEY CONCEPTS

### Definition
A teratogen is any substance that interferes with normal fetal development, leading to congenital abnormalities.

### Critical Period
- **Teratogenic period:** Day 31–71 from start of last menstrual period (~2–8 weeks post-conception)
- = Period of **organogenesis** (most vulnerable to structural malformations)
- First trimester = highest risk for major structural birth defects
- Later exposure: can cause organ dysfunction, growth restriction, behavioral effects

### Most Common Teratogenic Effects
- Neural tube defects
- Congenital heart abnormalities
- Cleft lip or palate
- Fetal stillbirth
- Skeletal abnormalities

### Prevalence of Drug-Related Birth Defects
- Medications account for only **2–3%** of all birth defects
- 97–98% from other causes (genetic, environmental, unknown)

## FDA PREGNANCY LABELING

### Old System (A/B/C/D/X) — Still Used for Drugs Approved Before 2015

| Category | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **A** | Controlled human studies: no fetal risk | Folic acid, levothyroxine |
| **B** | Animal studies: no risk; no adequate human studies OR animal risk not confirmed in humans | Acetaminophen, amoxicillin, metformin |
| **C** | Animal studies show adverse effects; no adequate human studies; benefits may outweigh risks | Tramadol, gabapentin, fluconazole |
| **D** | Positive evidence of human fetal risk; benefits may still outweigh risks in serious conditions | Tetracyclines, lithium, phenytoin, valproate |
| **X** | Demonstrated fetal abnormalities; risks clearly outweigh benefits; CONTRAINDICATED | Thalidomide, warfarin, isotretinoin, methotrexate, statins |

### New System (PLLR) — Pregnancy and Lactation Labeling Rule (2015)
Replaces A/B/C/D/X with three narrative subsections:
1. **Pregnancy** subsection: pregnancy exposure registry, risks in humans, clinical considerations, data
2. **Lactation** subsection: breastfeeding timing, drug excretion in milk, infant risks
3. **Females and Males of Reproductive Potential** subsection: contraception, infertility, testing

Limitation of old categories: Does not indicate which trimester is affected; does not convey risk-benefit balance; misleadingly implies alphabetical order = safety order.

## TERATOGENIC MEDICATIONS — BY CLASS (StatPearls + PMC Data)

### ABSOLUTELY CONTRAINDICATED (High Teratogenic Risk)

#### Isotretinoin (Accutane) — Category X
- Vitamin A derivative for severe acne
- Associated with: craniofacial defects, cardiac defects, CNS abnormalities, intellectual disability
- iPLEDGE program required (mandatory contraception + negative pregnancy tests)
- Avoid pregnancy during treatment and 1 month after last dose

#### Warfarin (Coumadin) — Category X / High Risk
- Crosses placenta; inhibits clotting factors in fetus + causes intracranial hemorrhage
- **Fetal warfarin syndrome:** nasal hypoplasia, stippled calcification of epiphyses, skeletal abnormalities
- Risk highest during weeks **6–9** of gestation
- Can also cause choanal atresia or stenosis, CNS malformations
- **Alternative in pregnancy:** Low molecular weight heparin (LMWH) — does NOT cross placenta

#### Methotrexate (MTX) — Category X
- Folic acid antagonist → severe neural tube defects, craniofacial abnormalities, limb defects
- Also used to treat ectopic pregnancy (kills trophoblast)
- Avoid pregnancy for at least 3 months after last dose (ACOG)

#### Thalidomide — Category X
- Classic teratogen: causes phocomelia (limb reduction defects)
- Exposed fetal gestational window: days 20–36 post-conception
- Also: ear abnormalities, heart defects, renal agenesis

#### Valproic Acid / Valproate — Category D / High Risk
- Antiepileptic
- Neural tube defects (spina bifida): 1–2% risk
- Fetal anticonvulsant syndrome: craniofacial, limb, cardiac defects
- Cognitive impairment in children exposed in utero
- Avoid in pregnancy; if necessary, use with folic acid 4–5 mg/day

#### Phenytoin (Dilantin) — Category D
- **Fetal hydantoin syndrome:** craniofacial, limb, cardiac defects, intellectual disability
- Use lower effective dose; monitor drug levels

#### ACE Inhibitors / ARBs — Category D in 2nd/3rd trimester (Category C in 1st)
- First trimester: increased risk of cardiovascular malformations (controversial)
- 2nd/3rd trimester: **fetal renal dysfunction, oligohydramnios, skull hypoplasia, fetal death**
- CONTRAINDICATED in 2nd and 3rd trimesters
- Safe alternative: labetalol, nifedipine, hydralazine for hypertension in pregnancy

#### Statins — Category X
- Inhibit cholesterol synthesis needed for fetal development
- Associated with: CNS defects, limb defects
- Discontinue before planned pregnancy and throughout pregnancy
- Examples: atorvastatin, simvastatin, rosuvastatin

#### Radioactive Iodine (I-131) — Contraindicated
- Ablates fetal thyroid (which concentrates iodide from ~12 weeks)
- Causes fetal hypothyroidism

#### Misoprostol — Risk with improper use
- Used medically for miscarriage management (safe in that context)
- Inappropriate use to induce abortion: associated with Möbius syndrome (facial nerve palsy)

#### Fluoroquinolones (Ciprofloxacin, Levofloxacin) — Avoid
- Inhibit DNA gyrase → organ agenesis risk in animal studies
- Articular cartilage damage in animals
- Renal, cardiac, CNS toxicity
- Avoid throughout pregnancy; use safer alternatives

#### Tetracyclines (Doxycycline, Minocycline) — Avoid (Category D)
- Bind to calcium → deposits in developing bone and teeth
- Dental staining / hypoplasia
- Bone growth impairment
- Potential liver necrosis
- Contraindicated especially after 2nd trimester (when teeth forming)

#### Trimethoprim-Sulfamethoxazole (TMP-SMX) — Avoid
- First trimester: fetal malformations risk (TMP is folate antagonist)
- After 32 weeks: kernicterus risk (sulfa displaces bilirubin from albumin)
- Safer UTI alternatives: nitrofurantoin (though avoid near term — hemolytic anemia), cephalexin, amoxicillin-clavulanate

#### Carbamazepine — Category D
- Neural tube defects, craniofacial abnormalities
- If used: supplement with high-dose folic acid

#### Lithium — Category D
- Ebstein anomaly (tricuspid valve defect) — historical concern; risk likely lower than previously thought
- Monitor levels carefully; dose adjustment needed in pregnancy

#### NSAIDs (especially after 20 weeks) — Category D 2nd/3rd trimester
- Premature closure of ductus arteriosus (esp. after 32 weeks)
- Oligohydramnios
- Indomethacin: used as tocolytic before 32 weeks (short-term, careful monitoring)
- Avoid all NSAIDs after 32 weeks; caution after 20 weeks (FDA 2020 warning)

#### Antithyroid Drugs
- **Methimazole (MMZ):** Teratogenic in first trimester (choanal atresia, aplasia cutis, MMZ embryopathy)
  - Use PTU in first trimester; switch to MMZ in second/third
- **PTU:** Less teratogenic but associated with maternal hepatotoxicity
  - Preferred in first trimester and for thyroid storm

### MEDICATIONS GENERALLY CONSIDERED SAFER IN PREGNANCY

#### Antibiotics — Safe
- **Penicillins** (amoxicillin, ampicillin): Category B — safe
- **Cephalosporins** (cephalexin, cefazolin): Category B — safe
- **Azithromycin:** Generally accepted; Category B
- **Erythromycin:** Generally safe; avoid estolate form (hepatotoxicity)
- **Clindamycin:** Generally safe
- **Nitrofurantoin:** Safe for UTI in 1st and 2nd trimester; avoid near term (hemolytic anemia risk)
- **Metronidazole:** Category B (do NOT use in first trimester per some guidelines; Category C per others)

#### Analgesics
- **Acetaminophen (paracetamol):** Generally safe; use lowest effective dose; some newer data suggest possible association with ADHD at high doses (under investigation)
- **Avoid:** Aspirin at full doses (GI bleeding, platelet issues); safe in low-dose (81 mg) for PE prevention, antiphospholipid syndrome
- **Avoid NSAIDs** (see above after 20 weeks)
- **Opioids:** Category C; risk of neonatal opioid withdrawal syndrome (NOWS), neonatal abstinence syndrome (NAS); use only if essential

#### Antiemetics (for HG)
- **Doxylamine + pyridoxine (B6):** Category A; FDA-approved for NVP
- **Promethazine:** Category C
- **Metoclopramide:** Category B
- **Ondansetron:** Category B (watch for QT prolongation; possible risk of cleft palate at very high doses — data uncertain)
- **Prochlorperazine:** Category C
- **Corticosteroids** (for HG): avoid first trimester if possible; small risk of oral clefts

#### Antihypertensives — Safe in Pregnancy
- **Labetalol** (alpha + beta blocker): Most used; Category C; safe
- **Nifedipine** (CCB, extended release): Safe; Category C; widely used for hypertension + tocolysis
- **Hydralazine:** Category C; safe; used for hypertensive urgency
- **Methyldopa:** Category B; traditionally used; somewhat less effective
- **Avoid:** ACE inhibitors, ARBs (2nd/3rd trimester), atenolol (associated with IUGR)

#### Anticoagulants
- **LMWH (enoxaparin, dalteparin):** Safe; does NOT cross placenta; preferred in pregnancy for DVT/PE and antiphospholipid syndrome
- **Unfractionated heparin (UFH):** Does not cross placenta; safe
- **Warfarin (Coumadin):** Contraindicated (especially weeks 6–12 and near term)
- **Direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs):** Avoid in pregnancy (limited data; likely harmful)

#### Corticosteroids
- **Betamethasone / Dexamethasone:** Cross placenta; used for fetal lung maturation (24–34 weeks preterm birth)
- **Prednisolone / Prednisone:** Minimally cross placenta; used for maternal conditions (asthma, autoimmune, PG)
- Risk: Cleft palate (first trimester, controversial); growth restriction; neonatal adrenal suppression with prolonged use

#### Antiepileptics — Comparative Safety
| Drug | Risk Level | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Valproate | HIGHEST | Neural tube defects; avoid if possible |
| Carbamazepine | High | NTD risk; use high-dose folate |
| Phenytoin | High | Fetal hydantoin syndrome |
| Lamotrigine | Lower | Preferred in pregnancy; dose adjustment needed |
| Levetiracetam | Lower | Increasingly used; limited long-term data |
| Oxcarbazepine | Moderate | Hyponatremia risk |

## PRINCIPLES FOR SAFE PRESCRIBING IN PREGNANCY
1. Always weigh benefit (to mother) vs. risk (to fetus)
2. Use the lowest effective dose for the shortest duration
3. Consider timing: first trimester is highest risk for structural malformations
4. Use drugs with the longest safety record when possible
5. Avoid polypharmacy
6. Consult specialized resources:
   - **NIH LactMed** (breastfeeding safety): https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK501922/
   - **TERIS** (Teratogen Information System): depts.washington.edu/terisweb/teris/
   - **Motherisk Program** (evidence-based teratogen counseling)
   - **MotherToBaby**: https://mothertobaby.org/ (free evidence-based fact sheets)
   - **REPROTOX:** reprotox.org (subscription database)
