# Pemphigoid Gestationis (PG)
## Source: StatPearls / NCBI Bookshelf (NIH) — Last Updated February 12, 2024
## Authors: Michael Fong, Grishma R. Gandhi, Amani Gharbi, Wissem Hafsi
## PMID: 29261895 | URL: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK470287/

---

## OVERVIEW
Pemphigoid gestationis (PG), formerly called "herpes gestationis" (a misnomer — not viral in etiology), is a rare, self-limiting, autoimmune subepidermal bullous dermatosis of pregnancy. First described in 1872 by Laws Milton. It shares clinical and pathogenic features with bullous pemphigoid.

---

## ETIOLOGY
- Caused by maternal autoimmune response against **hemidesmosomal proteins**:
  - **Bullous Pemphigoid 180 (BP180)** — also known as BPAG2 / Collagen XVII
  - **Bullous Pemphigoid 230 (BP230)** — also known as BPAG1
  - These proteins are located in the dermal basement membrane zone and placental tissue
- The maternal immune system misidentifies BP180 and BP230 as foreign proteins
- Precise trigger for autoantibody formation is not well understood
- Strongly associated with maternal HLA haplotypes:
  - **HLA-DRB1\*0301 (HLA-DR3)**
  - **HLA-DRB1\*0401/040X (HLA-DR4)**
  - Indicates vital role of MHC class II in pathogenesis
- Also associated with human leukocyte antigens PG3 and PG4

---

## EPIDEMIOLOGY
- Incidence: variably estimated from **1 in 2,000 to 1 in 60,000 pregnancies** (most sources: ~1/50,000)
- Worldwide distribution, no ethnic differences
- More prevalent in **multiparous** than primiparous women
- Recurrence in subsequent pregnancies: **30–50%**, typically with earlier onset and more severe course
- Rare association with hydatidiform moles and choriocarcinomas

---

## PATHOPHYSIOLOGY
1. Loss of maternal immunotolerance to placenta/fetus
2. Maternal IgG autoantibodies produced against BP180 and BP230 (expressed in skin, placental cytotrophoblastic cells, syncytiotrophoblastic cells, amniotic membrane epithelial cells)
3. Autoimmunization due to intolerance to BP180 placental antigen → local allogeneic reaction against fetoplacental unit
4. Abnormal expression of placental HLA class II antigens promotes this process
5. Alterations in placental basement membrane occur
6. Immune response at dermoepidermal junction → disruption of basement membrane → bullous skin lesions

---

## HISTOPATHOLOGY
- **Pre-bullous stage:** papillary edema, dermal infiltration (lymphocytes, histiocytes, variable eosinophils)
- **Bullous stage:** subepidermal blistering
- Characterized by: eosinophilic spongiosis, eosinophilic epitheliotropism, dense superficial dermal eosinophils
- Histologic examination alone NOT sufficient for diagnosis (findings not specific to PG)

---

## CLINICAL FEATURES

### Symptoms
- **Pruritus** is the primary symptom — may precede skin manifestations
- Polymorphic eruptions that evolve through stages

### Lesion progression
1. Eczematous or erythema multiforme-like lesions
2. Erythematous urticarial plaques and papules
3. Vesicles → tense blisters → bullae (in >65% of cases)

### Distribution (classic)
- Begins in **periumbilical area** (in ~90% of patients)
- Spreads to abdomen, extremities (thighs, palms, soles)
- **Face and mucous membranes typically SPARED**

### Clinical Course
- Can occur in any trimester or postpartum period (most common: third trimester)
- Flares common during parturition and postpartum
- Usually resolves spontaneously **1–2 months after delivery**
- Can exacerbate postpartum due to sudden antibody increase
- Can recur with menstrual cycles or oral contraceptive use (less common)

---

## FETAL RISKS
- Fetal growth restriction (IUGR / small-for-gestational-age)
- Preterm labor
- **Neonatal pemphigoid** in ~10% of affected pregnancies (transplacental passage of maternal antibodies) — temporary, usually resolves weeks after birth
- **No increased risk of stillbirth or miscarriage** (unlike some other dermatoses)
- Fetal risk correlates with disease severity

---

## MATERNAL COMPLICATIONS
- Grave's disease (associated autoimmune risk — most significant long-term maternal complication)
- Debilitating pruritus
- Long-term corticosteroid complications (see Treatment section)

---

## DIAGNOSIS / EVALUATION

### Gold Standard
- **Direct Immunofluorescence (DIF)** of skin biopsy — most sensitive and specific
  - Linear C3 deposits at basement membrane zone → **DIAGNOSTIC** (present in 100% of PG cases)
  - IgG autoantibodies also present
  - DIF may remain positive 6 months to 4 years after clinical remission

### Additional Tests
- **Indirect Immunofluorescence (IIF):** detects circulating IgG antibodies in serum of ~30–100% of PG patients
- **ELISA:** identifies BP180 IgG antibodies in serum; sensitivity and specificity ~95%; may distinguish PG from other pregnancy dermatoses; potentially may replace DIF as reference standard
- **Immunochemistry:** shows C4d deposits along dermal-epidermal junction
- **Routine histopathology:** helpful to exclude other conditions, but not specific/sufficient alone

### Limitations of DIF/IIF
- Requires non-formalin tissue fixation
- Needs appropriately equipped laboratory
- Increased cost — difficult in developing countries

## DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS
- Urticaria
- Allergic contact dermatitis
- Bullous pemphigoid
- Cicatricial pemphigoid
- Dermatitis herpetiformis
- Erythema multiforme
- Impetigo
- Intrahepatic cholestasis of pregnancy (ICP)
- Linear IgA dermatosis
- Bullous drug eruption
- Atopic eruption of pregnancy
- **Polymorphic Eruption of Pregnancy / PUPPP** (most challenging differential — see separate file)
  - Key distinction: PEP has negative DIF, no umbilical involvement; PG has positive C3 on DIF

## TREATMENT / MANAGEMENT

### Goal
- Relieve itching
- Prevent formation of new blisters

### Mild/Localized Disease
- **Topical high-potency corticosteroids** (first-line)
- **Oral antihistamines**

### Moderate-to-Severe Disease (>10% BSA, or unresponsive to topical therapy)
- **Oral prednisolone 0.5 mg/kg/day** (total daily dose <20 mg recommended)
- Preferred over other steroids because reduced concentration crosses placenta
- Taper if no new blisters within 2 weeks

### Refractory Disease
- **Intravenous immunoglobulins (IVIG):** better safety profile, used alone or adjunctively; shown effective in case studies
- **Cyclosporine** — risk: preterm birth, liver impairment
- **Dapsone** — risk: bone marrow suppression
- **Azathioprine** — risk: bone marrow suppression, liver impairment

### Emerging/Novel Therapies
- **Dupilumab** (anti-IL-4 receptor alpha monoclonal antibody): used in refractory/corticosteroid-dependent cases, especially with comorbidities like diabetes; associated with rapid remission and ability to wean steroids
- **Rituximab:** effective preconceptually to prevent recurrent PG; avoid subsequent pregnancy for 12 months after last infusion

### Corticosteroid Complications in Pregnancy
- Preterm birth
- Preeclampsia
- Preterm premature rupture of membranes (PPROM)
- Venous thromboembolism (VTE)
- Possible congenital anomalies
- Pyelonephritis
- Potentially intrauterine fetal death
- Small-for-gestational-age (unclear: underlying condition vs. steroid)
- Abnormal placentation (animal data)

---

## PROGNOSIS
- Overall prognosis: good
- High recurrence risk in subsequent pregnancies
- Increased risk of Grave's disease (and other autoimmune diseases) in the mother
- ~10% of affected pregnancies → neonatal pemphigoid (self-resolving)

---
