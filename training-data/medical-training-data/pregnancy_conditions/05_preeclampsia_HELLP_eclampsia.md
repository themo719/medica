## SPECTRUM OF HYPERTENSIVE DISORDERS OF PREGNANCY

Hypertensive disorders of pregnancy (HDP) are a **leading cause of maternal and perinatal mortality worldwide**. They represent a spectrum:

```
Gestational Hypertension → Preeclampsia (without severe features) → Preeclampsia (with severe features) → Eclampsia
                                                                                          ↓
                                                                                  HELLP Syndrome
```

- Affects 2–8% of pregnancy-related complications worldwide
- >50,000 maternal deaths and >500,000 fetal deaths worldwide

---

## 1. GESTATIONAL HYPERTENSION

### Diagnosis (ACOG criteria)
- High blood pressure, **no protein in urine**, no other signs of organ damage
- Blood pressure levels were normal before pregnancy
- Onset after **20 weeks of gestation**
- Resolves within 12 weeks postpartum

### Note
- Can progress to preeclampsia (40% risk if initial diagnosis is transient gestational hypertension)
- Fetal monitoring via CTG only if clinically indicated

---

## 2. PREECLAMPSIA

### Definition
- New-onset hypertension usually after 20 weeks gestation (frequently near term)
- Most often with proteinuria
- Affects 2–8% of pregnancies globally

### Diagnosis of MILD preeclampsia (ACOG):
- Systolic BP ≥140 mmHg OR diastolic BP ≥90 mmHg
- PLUS one of:
  - Urine protein ≥0.3 g in 24-hour specimen
  - Protein:creatinine ratio >0.3
  - Urine dipstick ≥2+ (used only if quantitative measures unavailable)

### Diagnosis of SEVERE preeclampsia (severe features include):
- Systolic BP ≥160 mmHg OR diastolic BP ≥110 mmHg on two occasions 4 hours apart
- Thrombocytopenia: platelets <100,000/μL
- Renal insufficiency: creatinine >1.1 mg/dL or doubling of baseline
- Impaired liver function: elevated liver enzymes (2x ULN), severe right upper quadrant/epigastric pain
- Pulmonary edema
- New-onset headache unresponsive to medication or visual symptoms
- Fetal growth restriction

### When BP is ≥160/110 mmHg:
- Requires urgent treatment in monitored setting
- Increased risk of severe end-organ dysfunction even at minimally elevated BP

### Pathophysiology
- Defective placentation → insufficient spiral artery remodeling → placental ischemia
- Release of antiangiogenic factors (sFlt-1) → vascular endothelial dysfunction
- Widespread vasospasm and endothelial damage
- Placental weight often <10th percentile
- Associated with MVM (maternal vascular malperfusion) lesions
- Definitive treatment: delivery (removes inciting placental factors)

### Atypical Presentations
- Normotensive preeclampsia (proteinuria with severe features, no hypertension)
- Preeclampsia superimposed on chronic hypertension
- Postpartum preeclampsia (up to 6 weeks after delivery)

### Angiogenic Markers (when available)
- sFlt-1 / PlGF ratio: marker of uteroplacental dysfunction
- Recommended in addition to clinical assessment for suspected preeclampsia <37 weeks

### Differential Diagnosis
- Chronic hypertension
- Gestational hypertension
- Antiphospholipid antibody syndrome
- Thrombotic microangiopathies
- Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE)
- Epilepsy / seizure disorder
- Chronic renal disease / chronic liver disease
- Pheochromocytoma or other endocrinopathies

### Treatment
- **Definitive: delivery**
- Antihypertensives: labetalol, nifedipine, hydralazine
- Magnesium sulfate: seizure prophylaxis (severe preeclampsia) and treatment (eclampsia)
- If <34 weeks and delivery indicated → antenatal steroids for fetal lung maturation (do NOT delay delivery)
- Also applies to late preterm (34–36+6 weeks)
- Prevention: low-dose aspirin for high-risk women + calcium supplementation

### Indications for Delivery (Maternal)
- Uncontrolled BP despite repeated medical management
- Persistent headache/visual disturbance
- Right upper quadrant/epigastric pain despite treatment
- Myocardial infarction, stroke
- Pulmonary edema
- HELLP syndrome
- Eclampsia
- Suspected placental abruption

---

## 3. ECLAMPSIA

- One or more seizures in the context of preeclampsia, during pregnancy or postpartum
- Very serious complication
- Treatment: IV magnesium sulfate (treatment and prevention of further seizures)
- Prompt delivery indicated after stabilization

---

## 4. HELLP SYNDROME

### Definition (acronym)
- **H** = Hemolysis
- **EL** = Elevated Liver enzymes
- **LP** = Low Platelets

### Epidemiology
- Complicates 0.5–0.9% of all pregnancies
- Affects ~15% of women with preeclampsia/eclampsia
- ~45,000 women per year in the United States
- Usually occurs in third trimester (28–40 weeks), but can occur anytime after 20 weeks
- Can develop up to 7 days after delivery

### Risk Factors
- Preeclampsia or eclampsia (up to 1 in 5 women with these develop HELLP)
- Previous HELLP syndrome
- Age >35
- Multiparous
- White race (some studies)
- Chronic hypertension, kidney disease, diabetes
- Autoimmune conditions, clotting disorders
- Family history

### Note: ~15% of HELLP cases have NO clinical manifestations of preeclampsia (atypical HELLP)

### Diagnostic Criteria (Tennessee Classification — most used)
- **Hemolysis:** abnormal peripheral smear, LDH >600 IU/L, bilirubin >1.2 mg/dL
- **Elevated liver enzymes:** AST >70 IU/L
- **Low platelets:** <100,000/μL

### Mississippi Classification (alternative)
- Class 1: platelets <50,000/μL
- Class 2: platelets 50,000–100,000/μL
- Class 3: platelets 100,000–150,000/μL

### Symptoms
- Right upper quadrant or epigastric pain (most common)
- Nausea/vomiting
- Malaise
- Headache
- Visual changes
- Edema
- May initially resemble gastroenteritis or flu

### Maternal Complications
- Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)
- Postpartum hemorrhage (PPH)
- Kidney failure
- Liver hemorrhage or rupture
- Liver failure
- Pulmonary edema
- Placental abruption
- Stroke

### Fetal/Neonatal Complications
- Preterm birth (majority of cases)
- Babies >1000 g: survival rate similar to non-HELLP of comparable size
- Babies <1000 g: worse outcomes, longer hospitalizations, more ventilator support

### Stillbirth Statistics (HELLP)
- Stillbirth rate in developed countries: 51 per 1,000 HELLP pregnancies
- Overall infant death rate: 7.7–60% (from abruption, intrauterine asphyxia, prematurity)

### Treatment
- **Definitive: delivery** (especially ≥34 weeks)
- Magnesium sulfate for seizure prophylaxis
- Antihypertensives for BP control
- Corticosteroids (betamethasone or dexamethasone) to improve platelet count and fetal lung maturation if <34 weeks
- Blood products if DIC or severe bleeding
- Intensive monitoring

### Recurrence Risk
- Recurrent HELLP: 2–19%
- Recurrent preeclampsia in subsequent pregnancies: 16–52% (higher if HELLP onset in second trimester or if chronic hypertension)

---

## POSTPARTUM MONITORING
- Preeclampsia/HELLP can manifest or worsen postpartum
- Monitor BP, symptoms, labs up to 6 weeks postpartum
- Women with hx of preeclampsia: increased lifetime risk of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes
