# Hyperemesis Gravidarum (HG)

## 1. StatPearls — Vadakekut ES, Mahdy H. NBK532917. Last Updated July 6, 2025. PMID 30422512

---

## OVERVIEW
Hyperemesis gravidarum (HG) represents the **severe end of the spectrum** of nausea and vomiting of pregnancy (NVP). It is a **leading cause of first-trimester hospitalization**. Unlike typical NVP (affects ~70–80% of pregnancies), HG involves persistent, pathological vomiting that can continue throughout the entire pregnancy.

---

## EPIDEMIOLOGY
- Affects approximately **0.3–3%** of pregnancies
- Common cause of early pregnancy hospitalization and emergency department visits
- HG recurs in subsequent pregnancies (high recurrence rate)
- Affects pregnant individuals across all demographics

---

## ETIOLOGY & PATHOGENESIS
- **Multifactorial, not fully understood**
- Key hypotheses:

### 1. hCG Role (most implicated)
- Peak of NVP and hCG coincide at ~9–12 weeks gestation
- Conditions with elevated hCG (multiple gestation, molar pregnancy) have higher rates of HG
- hCG has cross-reactivity with thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH) receptors → can cause gestational transient thyrotoxicosis
- hCG stimulates placental prostaglandin E2 (PGE2) release → PGE2 causes gastric smooth muscle relaxation and delayed motility

### 2. Hormonal changes
- Elevated progesterone → reduced GI tract motility → slower gastric/intestinal transit
- Increased placental gastrin → enhanced gastric acid secretion
- Estrogen: may have a role

### 3. Immunological factors
- Immune dysregulation during early pregnancy

### 4. GI/anatomical contributions
- Enlarging uterus → shifts abdominal organs, increases intra-abdominal pressure
- Reduction in lower esophageal sphincter tone → GERD

### 5. Gestational transient thyrotoxicosis
- ~10% of HG patients
- Caused by TSH receptor stimulation by hCG
- Self-limiting, generally does NOT require treatment

---

## CLINICAL PRESENTATION

### Onset
- Typically begins **4–6 weeks gestation**
- Peaks at **8–12 weeks** then usually improves (second trimester)
- In HG: persists well beyond, can last entire gestation or into postpartum

### Windsor Definition (international consensus, current best practice)
- Diagnosis in first 16 weeks when:
  - Nausea and vomiting
  - At least ONE is severe
  - Impairs sufficient intake
  - Affects activities of daily living
- **Ketonuria is NOT required** for diagnosis or treatment eligibility (older criteria required ketonuria — now outdated)

### Classic Features
- Persistent, continual vomiting
- Weight loss ≥5% of pre-pregnancy weight
- Dehydration
- Metabolic disturbances (electrolyte imbalances)
- Ketonuria (may be present but not required for diagnosis)
- Orthostatic hypotension

### Nutritional/Metabolic Complications
- **Wernicke encephalopathy** (thiamine/B1 deficiency) — serious neurological complication
- Other nutritional deficiencies:
  - Vitamin B1 (thiamine)
  - Vitamin B12
  - Vitamin K
  - Hyponatremia, hypokalemia, hypochloremia
  - Hypomagnesemia

---

## DIAGNOSIS
- Clinical diagnosis by **exclusion**
- Supported by: ketonuria, orthostatic hypotension, electrolyte imbalances
- Rule out other causes of vomiting:
  - Appendicitis
  - Gastroenteritis
  - Cholecystitis
  - Pancreatitis
  - Urinary tract infection / pyelonephritis
  - Hepatitis
  - Peptic ulcer disease
  - Molar pregnancy / choriocarcinoma (high hCG states)
  - Hyperthyroidism (check TSH, free T4)
  - Diabetic ketoacidosis

### History to obtain:
- Pregnancy status and gestational age
- History of complications in prior pregnancies
- Frequency and severity of nausea/vomiting
- Interventions tried and their outcomes
- Onset, nature, associated symptoms

---

## IMPACT

### On mother:
- Dehydration and malnutrition
- Electrolyte imbalances
- Psychological distress (anxiety, depression)
- Reduced quality of life
- Wernicke encephalopathy (if thiamine deficiency)
- Esophageal rupture (Boerhaave syndrome) — rare

### On offspring:
- Short-term: preterm birth risk, low birth weight
- Long-term adverse outcomes (under investigation)
- Note: With adequate management, HG not typically associated with adverse fetal outcomes

### Interaction with Gestational Diabetes Screening:
- HG in first trimester can cause false positive GDM screening (starvation changes glucose metabolism)
- First trimester positive predictive value for GDM lower in HG patients (28.5% vs 72.7%)
- Actual GDM rates in second trimester similar to controls
- Clinicians should be aware of pseudo-GDM diagnosis risk in HG patients

---

## TREATMENT

### Hydration
- IV fluids (normal saline with dextrose, Ringer's lactate)
- Correct electrolyte imbalances

### Thiamine supplementation
- **MANDATORY before dextrose administration** to prevent Wernicke encephalopathy
- IV thiamine 100 mg before any dextrose infusion

### Antiemetics (evidence-based)
- **First-line:**
  - Doxylamine + pyridoxine (Vitamin B6) — safe in pregnancy (FDA-approved)
  - Ginger (mild NVP, less evidence for HG)
- **Second-line:**
  - Promethazine
  - Metoclopramide
  - Ondansetron (commonly used; some concern for cardiac defects — limited data, generally considered acceptable for severe HG)
  - Prochlorperazine
  - Chlorpromazine
- **Third-line (refractory):**
  - Corticosteroids (methylprednisolone): some evidence for benefit; avoid in first trimester if possible (small risk of oral clefts)
  - Droperidol
  - Diazepam

### Nutrition
- Small, frequent, bland meals
- Avoid triggers (strong odors, spicy/fatty food)
- Enteral nutrition (nasogastric/nasojejunal tube) if unable to maintain oral intake
- Total parenteral nutrition (TPN) in severe refractory cases (last resort)

### Discharge criteria
- Tolerating oral fluids
- Symptoms controlled
- Electrolytes normalized

---

## PROGNOSIS
- Most cases resolve by end of first/early second trimester
- With proper management, maternal and fetal outcomes generally good
- HG in prior pregnancy = high risk for recurrence
- Untreated severe HG can be life-threatening
