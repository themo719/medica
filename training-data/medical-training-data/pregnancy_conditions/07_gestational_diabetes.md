## DEFINITION
Gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM) is **glucose intolerance first recognized during pregnancy**. It is one of the most common medical complications of pregnancy. GDM poses significant short- and long-term risks to both mother and fetus.

---

## EPIDEMIOLOGY
- Prevalence: varies by population and diagnostic criteria used; estimated 3–14% of pregnancies globally
- Risk higher in: South/Southeast Asian, Hispanic, African American, Native American, Pacific Islander populations
- Increasing prevalence due to rising rates of obesity, sedentary lifestyle, delayed childbearing
- Most significant associations for GDM risk: family history, previous GDM, Southeast Asian ethnicity, parity, high BMI

---

## PATHOGENESIS
- **Insulin resistance** increases physiologically during pregnancy (due to placental hormones: human placental lactogen, progesterone, cortisol, prolactin)
- Pancreatic beta cells normally compensate with increased insulin secretion
- GDM occurs when pancreatic beta cell function is insufficient to overcome increased insulin resistance
- Women with GDM have underlying reduced beta cell reserve revealed by pregnancy
- Placental hormones (especially human placental lactogen / hPL) are key drivers

---

## RISK FACTORS
- Obesity (BMI ≥25 kg/m², or ≥23 in Asian Americans)
- Previous GDM
- Family history of type 2 diabetes (first-degree relative)
- Previous macrosomic baby (birth weight ≥4000–4500 g)
- Previous stillbirth or unexplained perinatal loss
- High-risk ethnicity (African American, Hispanic, Native American, South/Southeast Asian, Pacific Islander)
- Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)
- Advanced maternal age (>35)
- Prediabetes (HbA1c 5.7–6.4%)
- Multiple gestation
- Excessive gestational weight gain

---

## SCREENING

### Standard Universal Screening (ACOG, ADA)
- **24–28 weeks gestation** for all pregnant individuals not previously diagnosed

### Two Approaches:

#### One-Step Approach (IADPSG/ADA preferred):
- **75-g oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT)** after 8-hour fast
- GDM diagnosed if ANY of the following met:
  - Fasting glucose ≥92 mg/dL (5.1 mmol/L)
  - 1-hour glucose ≥180 mg/dL (10.0 mmol/L)
  - 2-hour glucose ≥153 mg/dL (8.5 mmol/L)

#### Two-Step Approach (ACOG preferred for universal screening):
- **Step 1:** 50-g glucose challenge test (non-fasting), 1-hour
  - Positive if ≥130–140 mg/dL (threshold varies by institution)
- **Step 2 (if positive):** 100-g OGTT after 8-hour fast
  - GDM diagnosed if ≥2 values met (Carpenter and Coustan criteria):
    - Fasting ≥95 mg/dL
    - 1-hour ≥180 mg/dL
    - 2-hour ≥155 mg/dL
    - 3-hour ≥140 mg/dL

### Early Screening (High-Risk Patients)
- **ACOG 2024 update:** Screen early in pregnancy if BMI ≥30 kg/m² AND ≥1 risk factor
  - Options: 75-g or 50-g OGTT at first prenatal visit
  - If A1C ≥6.5%: pregestational diabetes (not GDM)
  - If A1C ≥5.7% or impaired glucose: counseling; standard screening at 24–28 wk still needed
- **ADA 2024:** Screen BMI >25 kg/m² (>23 in Asian Americans) + ≥1 risk factor at first prenatal visit

### Distinguishing GDM from Pre-existing Diabetes
- A1C ≥6.5% or fasting glucose ≥126 mg/dL or 2-hour OGTT ≥200 mg/dL early in pregnancy = overt pre-existing diabetes (not GDM)
- True GDM: glucose intolerance first recognized during pregnancy, not meeting overt diabetes criteria

## DIAGNOSIS CRITERIA COMPARISON

| Test | ACOG (2-step, Carpenter-Coustan) | IADPSG/ADA (1-step, 75g) |
|---|---|---|
| Fasting | ≥95 mg/dL | ≥92 mg/dL |
| 1-hour | ≥180 mg/dL | ≥180 mg/dL |
| 2-hour | ≥155 mg/dL | ≥153 mg/dL |
| 3-hour | ≥140 mg/dL | N/A |
| Positive threshold | ≥2 values | ≥1 value |

---

## GLYCEMIC TARGETS DURING PREGNANCY (ACOG)
- Fasting / preprandial: **<95 mg/dL**
- 1-hour postprandial: **<140 mg/dL**
- 2-hour postprandial: **<120 mg/dL**

## COMPLICATIONS

### Maternal
- Increased risk of preeclampsia / hypertensive disorders
- Cesarean delivery
- Development of type 2 diabetes postpartum (lifetime risk ~50–70%)
- Cardiovascular disease long-term
- Recurrence of GDM in subsequent pregnancies (~50%)

### Fetal/Neonatal
- Macrosomia (large for gestational age, birth weight ≥4000 g) → birth trauma, shoulder dystocia
- Neonatal hypoglycemia
- Neonatal respiratory distress syndrome
- Hyperbilirubinemia
- Polycythemia
- Small for gestational age (if poorly perfused placenta)
- Stillbirth (especially with uncontrolled diabetes)
- Childhood obesity and metabolic syndrome

### Connection to Preeclampsia
- GDM increases risk of preeclampsia 2–4x
- Mechanism: hyperglycemia inhibits trophoblast migration/invasion → spiral artery remodeling deficiency → placental ischemia → angiogenic imbalance → vascular endothelial injury

---

## TREATMENT

### Step 1: Lifestyle / Non-Pharmacologic (First-Line)
- **Medical Nutrition Therapy (MNT):** individualized diet plan
  - Carbohydrate restriction (complex, distributed throughout the day)
  - Adequate calories for pregnancy weight gain
- **Physical activity:** 30 min moderate-intensity aerobic exercise ≥5 days/week (minimum 150 min/week)
  - Walking 10–15 min after each meal particularly effective

### Step 2: Pharmacologic (if lifestyle insufficient within 1–2 weeks)

#### Insulin (ACOG/ADA preferred first-line pharmacotherapy)
- Preferred due to inability to cross placenta in significant amounts
- **Short-acting analogues:** insulin lispro, insulin aspart (preferred over regular insulin — faster onset)
- **Long-acting/intermediate:** NPH insulin (still used); insulin glargine and detemir available
- Divided doses: combination of basal and bolus insulin

#### Metformin
- SMFM endorses as reasonable first-choice in addition to insulin
- ACOG/ADA: insulin preferred; metformin acceptable if patient declines or cannot take insulin
- Crosses placenta — no long-term offspring studies available
- Monitor: risk of neonatal hypoglycemia lower than with glyburide

#### Glyburide
- **NOT recommended** (ACOG/ADA) — studies show worse outcomes including macrosomia and birth injury
- Should NOT be used in place of insulin

## DELIVERY TIMING (ACOG)
| Glucose Control | Recommended Delivery |
|---|---|
| Diet-controlled GDM | By 40 6/7 weeks |
| Medication-controlled GDM | 39 0/7 – 39 6/7 weeks |
| Uncontrolled GDM | 37 0/7 – 38 6/7 weeks |
| Uncontrolled + abnormal surveillance | Earlier, individualized |

- **Cesarean delivery:** discuss if estimated fetal weight ≥4,500 g

---

## POSTPARTUM FOLLOW-UP
- Screen for type 2 diabetes at **4–12 weeks postpartum** (75-g OGTT)
- Alternative: 75-g OGTT during delivery hospitalization (increases testing rate)
- Long-term: screen every 1–3 years for life (high lifetime risk of T2DM)
- GDM = marker for future metabolic syndrome, cardiovascular disease

---

## INTERACTION WITH OTHER PREGNANCY CONDITIONS
- Increased preeclampsia risk (shared pathophysiology)
- HG can cause false positive first-trimester GDM screening (starvation changes metabolism)
- GDM management complicated by nausea/vomiting (dose timing of insulin)
