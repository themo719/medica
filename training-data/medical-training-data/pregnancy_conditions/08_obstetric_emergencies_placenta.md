## PART 1: PLACENTAL ABRUPTION (PA)

### Definition
Premature separation of the placenta from the uterine wall **before completion of the second stage of labor**.

### Incidence
- Approximately **0.4–1.0%** of pregnancies
- Leading cause of bleeding in second half of pregnancy
- Incidence increasing despite improved obstetrical care (suggests multifactorial etiology)
- Recurrence rate: **3–10%**

### Pathophysiology
- Maternal vessels tear away from placenta
- Bleeding occurs between uterine lining and maternal side of placenta
- Sudden and unpredictable onset
- Can be concealed (retroplacental) or revealed (external bleeding)

### Risk Factors
- **Previous placental abruption** (strongest risk factor)
- Chronic hypertension: 3–4x increased risk
- Preeclampsia (especially with severe features): 4–6x increased risk
- Gestational diabetes mellitus
- Preterm premature rupture of membranes (PPROM)
- Chorioamnionitis
- Oligohydramnios
- Iron deficiency anemia
- Infection / inflammatory states
- Folate deficiency / hyperhomocysteinemia (folic acid supplementation → 19% lower risk)
- Multiple gestation
- Uterine fibroids
- Smoking and cocaine use (strong association)
- Trauma / abdominal injury
- Thrombophilia (antiphospholipid syndrome, prothrombin gene mutation)
- Advanced maternal age (>40)
- Short umbilical cord
- External cephalic version

### Clinical Presentation
- **Classic triad:** vaginal bleeding + abdominal/uterine pain + uterine contractions
- Sudden-onset severe abdominal pain (most common)
- Vaginal bleeding (may be absent in concealed abruption)
- Uterine tenderness / rigidity ("woody uterus")
- High uterine tone (hypertonia/tachysystole)
- Non-reassuring fetal heart tracing (variable/late decelerations, bradycardia)
- Signs of maternal shock if severe (tachycardia, hypotension)

### Diagnosis
- Primarily **clinical diagnosis**
- Ultrasound: can show retroplacental hematoma, but normal US does NOT rule out abruption
- CTG / electronic fetal monitoring: assess fetal status
- Labs: CBC (thrombocytopenia), coagulation studies (DIC risk), fibrinogen (<200 mg/dL suggests severe)

### Complications
- Maternal: hemorrhage, DIC, hysterectomy, maternal death
- Fetal/Neonatal: preterm birth, low birth weight, perinatal asphyxia, stillbirth, neonatal death

### Management
- Depends on gestational age and severity
- **Severe abruption at any gestational age:** immediate delivery (often cesarean)
- **Mild-moderate, stable, preterm:** possible expectant management with close monitoring
- Blood products/transfusion if hemorrhage
- Treat DIC if present
- Antenatal corticosteroids if <34 weeks and time permits

## PART 2: PLACENTA PREVIA (PP)

### Definition
Complete or partial covering of the internal os (cervical opening) by the placenta.

### Classification
- **Complete previa:** placenta completely covers internal os
- **Partial previa:** placenta partially covers internal os
- **Marginal previa:** placental edge at/near internal os
- **Low-lying placenta:** edge within 2 cm of internal os (may resolve)

### Incidence
- ~1% of births (varies by study)
- Diagnosis usually made by ultrasound

### Risk Factors
- Previous cesarean delivery (scar on uterus)
- Previous uterine surgery / myomectomy
- Multiple previous pregnancies (multiparity)
- Multiple gestation
- Advanced maternal age
- Smoking
- Prior placenta previa
- Uterine abnormalities

### Clinical Presentation
- **Classic:** painless vaginal bleeding in second or third trimester
- Often bright red blood
- No abdominal pain (contrast with abruption: painful)
- Abnormal fetal lie (oblique, transverse) due to low-lying placenta

### Differential Diagnosis of Third-Trimester Vaginal Bleeding
- Placenta previa (painless)
- Placental abruption (painful)
- Labor (bloody show)
- Vasa previa (fetal vessel rupture — emergency)
- Cervical pathology (cancer, cervicitis, polyp)

### Complications
- Postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) — major risk
- Placenta accreta spectrum (PAS):
  - Placenta accreta: abnormal adherence
  - Placenta increta: invades myometrium
  - Placenta percreta: penetrates serosa / adjacent organs
- Blood transfusion requirement
- Hysterectomy
- Maternal ICU admission
- Maternal death
- Preterm birth

### PPH Definition
Blood loss ≥1000 mL with signs/symptoms of hypovolemia within 24 hours of delivery

### Management
- **Vaginal delivery CONTRAINDICATED** in complete previa → cesarean delivery required
- Avoid digital vaginal exam (can precipitate bleeding)
- Speculum exam is safe if needed
- Pelvic rest (no intercourse, no tampons)
- Hospitalization if active bleeding
- If delivery needed preterm: antenatal corticosteroids for fetal lung maturity
- Planned cesarean at 36–37 weeks (stable, no bleeding)
- Emergency cesarean if uncontrolled bleeding

## PART 3: PRETERM LABOR (PTL)

### Definition
Regular uterine contractions + progressive cervical change before **37 0/7 weeks gestation**

### Epidemiology
- Preterm birth: ~10% of all births in the US
- Leading cause of neonatal morbidity and mortality worldwide

### Types
- **Spontaneous preterm labor (sPTL):** no identifiable cause
- **Indicated preterm birth:** medically indicated early delivery (e.g., preeclampsia, fetal compromise)

### Risk Factors for Spontaneous Preterm Labor
- Prior spontaneous preterm birth (strongest risk factor)
- Short cervical length (<25 mm by transvaginal US at 20–24 weeks)
- Cervical incompetence / cerclage history
- PPROM
- Infection (UTI, chorioamnionitis, bacterial vaginosis, STI)
- Multiple gestation
- Uterine anomalies
- Poor nutrition
- Substance use (smoking, cocaine)
- Extremes of maternal age (<17, >35)
- Low socioeconomic status
- Medical comorbidities: hypertension, diabetes, autoimmune disease

### Clinical Presentation
- Regular uterine contractions (≥4/hour, or ≥8/2 hours)
- Pelvic pressure
- Low back pain
- Vaginal discharge change
- Possible fluid leakage (PPROM)
- Bloody show

### Evaluation
1. Maternal vital signs
2. **Speculum exam BEFORE digital exam** (if PPROM suspected — digital exam increases infection risk)
3. Speculum exam: assess cervical dilation/effacement, membrane status, fluid pooling, discharge
4. CTG: assess contractions and fetal well-being
5. Cervical length by transvaginal ultrasound
6. **Fetal fibronectin (fFN):** high negative predictive value — if negative, delivery unlikely within 14 days
7. Labs: urine culture, vaginal cultures (GBS, STIs), CBC, consider amniocentesis if infection suspected

### Management
- **Corticosteroids:** betamethasone 12 mg IM × 2 doses, 24 hours apart (if 24–34 weeks; also 34–36 6/7 weeks per recent guidance) — for fetal lung maturation
- **Tocolysis:** delay delivery 48 hours to allow corticosteroid effect
  - Nifedipine (calcium channel blocker) — first-line
  - Indomethacin (NSAID) — before 32 weeks
  - Magnesium sulfate (neuroprotection for fetus <32 weeks, also tocolytic)
  - Terbutaline (beta-agonist) — used short-term only
- **Antibiotics:** if GBS positive, give penicillin (ampicillin if allergic) for intrapartum prophylaxis
- **Progesterone:** 17-hydroxyprogesterone caproate (17-OHPC) or vaginal progesterone for prevention in subsequent pregnancies with prior sPTB

## PART 4: PRETERM PREMATURE RUPTURE OF MEMBRANES (PPROM)

### Definition
- **PROM:** rupture of fetal membranes before onset of labor
- **PPROM:** rupture of fetal membranes before 37 weeks gestation AND before onset of labor
- **pPROM at <24 weeks:** previable PPROM — highest morbidity

### Epidemiology
- PROM causes ~1/3 of all preterm deliveries
- Complicates ~3% of pregnancies
- PPROM: associated with significant morbidity and mortality

### Pathophysiology
- Infection and inflammation: most common underlying cause
- Collagen degradation weakens membranes
- Increased matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs)
- Risk: chorioamnionitis → PPROm or vice versa

### Risk Factors
- Prior PPROM (most significant)
- Intra-amniotic infection
- Cervical incompetence
- Uterine overdistension (polyhydramnios, multiple gestation)
- Tobacco use
- Low BMI / nutritional deficiency
- Short cervical length
- Previous preterm birth
- STIs (chlamydia, gonorrhea)
- Bacterial vaginosis

### Clinical Presentation
- Sudden gush or continuous leakage of fluid from vagina
- Distinguish from urinary incontinence (common in pregnancy)
- Pooling of straw-colored fluid in posterior fornix on speculum exam

### Diagnosis
1. **Sterile speculum exam:** pooling, fluid from os, ferning on microscopy
2. **Nitrazine test:** amniotic fluid is alkaline (turns nitrazine paper blue); false positives with blood, urine, BV
3. **Ferning test:** dried amniotic fluid forms fern pattern under microscopy
4. **AmniSure (PAMG-1 test):** detects placental alpha-microglobulin-1; high sensitivity/specificity
5. **Actim PROM test:** detects phosphorylated insulin-like growth factor binding protein-1 (IGFBP-1)
6. Ultrasound: confirm oligohydramnios (AFI <5 cm or single deepest pocket <2 cm)
7. Amniocentesis: to rule out infection if diagnosis unclear

### Complications
- Chorioamnionitis (intraamniotic infection)
- Placental abruption
- Cord prolapse (if presenting part not engaged)
- Preterm birth and all associated neonatal complications
- Fetal growth restriction
- Pulmonary hypoplasia (if very early PPROM)
- Maternal sepsis

### Management
- **At ≥37 weeks:** deliver (PROM at term) — high risk of chorioamnionitis with expectant management
- **34–36+6 weeks PPROM:** delivery recommended (ACOG)
- **24–33+6 weeks PPROM:** expectant management with:
  - Antenatal corticosteroids (betamethasone)
  - Antibiotics (latency antibiotics: ampicillin + erythromycin → azithromycin replacing erythromycin per recent guidance)
  - Magnesium sulfate for neuroprotection if <32 weeks
  - GBS prophylaxis in labor
  - Close fetal and maternal monitoring
  - Hospitalization
- **<24 weeks (previable PPROM):** difficult decision; counsel about options:
  - Composite maternal morbidity with expectant management: ~60%
  - Discuss risks of infection, abruption, maternal morbidity
