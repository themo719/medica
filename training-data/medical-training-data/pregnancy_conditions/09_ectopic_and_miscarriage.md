## PART 1: ECTOPIC PREGNANCY

### Definition
Implantation of a fertilized ovum **outside the uterine cavity**.

### Incidence & Importance
- Affects **1–2%** of pregnancies in the United States
- **Leading cause of maternal mortality in the first trimester**
- Accounts for **2.7–4%** of pregnancy-related deaths
- US incidence has risen: 4.5 per 1,000 pregnancies (1970) → ~19.7 per 1,000 (1992)

### Location of Implantation
| Location | Frequency |
|---|---|
| Fallopian tube | ~97% (ampullary most common, then isthmic, fimbrial, cornual/interstitial) |
| Interstitial (cornual) | 2–4% — higher risk of life-threatening hemorrhage |
| Ovary | Rare |
| Cervix | Rare |
| Abdominal (retroperitoneal, liver, spleen, appendix, lung) | Very rare — usually found at surgery |
| Cesarean scar | Rare but increasing (scar pregnancy) |
| Heterotopic (IUP + ectopic simultaneously) | ~1/30,000 spontaneous; higher with IVF |

### Risk Factors
- **Previous ectopic pregnancy** (strongest risk factor — ~10x increased risk)
- Prior pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) — from gonorrhea, chlamydia
- Previous fallopian tube surgery or sterilization
- Prior pelvic/abdominal surgery (adhesions)
- Conception with IUD in place
- Progesterone-only contraceptives
- Assisted reproductive technology (IVF, IUI)
- Diethylstilbestrol (DES) exposure in utero (historical)
- Smoking
- Endometriosis

### Pathophysiology
- Most commonly due to underlying fallopian tube abnormalities
- Tubal damage → impaired transport of fertilized egg → implantation in tube
- Trophoblast invades tubal wall → can cause rupture and catastrophic hemorrhage

### Clinical Presentation
- Classic triad: **abdominal/pelvic pain + vaginal bleeding + amenorrhea**
- Pain: may be dull/aching (unruptured) → severe/sharp (tubal distension/rupture)
- Bleeding: usually light, spotting, brown (abnormal)
- Shoulder tip pain: suggests diaphragmatic irritation from hemoperitoneum
- Signs of rupture: severe peritoneal pain, guarding, rigidity, hemodynamic instability (tachycardia, hypotension, shock)
- Note: young, fit women can compensate hemodynamically until sudden decompensation
- **Atypical presentations are relatively common** — can mimic:
  - Appendicitis
  - Salpingitis / PID
  - Ruptured corpus luteum or follicular cyst
  - Threatened / inevitable spontaneous abortion
  - Ovarian torsion
  - UTI

### Diagnosis

#### β-hCG Patterns
| Pattern | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Viable IUP (initial β-hCG <1500) | 99% likelihood of ≥49% rise over 48 hours |
| Viable IUP | Doubles approximately every 2 days (up to ~10 weeks) |
| β-hCG ≥2000 mIU/mL + no IUP on TVUS | Highly suspicious for ectopic |
| β-hCG rise <35% in 48 hours | Suggests non-viable or ectopic (accuracy ~80%) |
| β-hCG decrease ≥21% in 48 hours | Suggests failed IUP or resolving ectopic |
| β-hCG falling but slowly | May be resolving ectopic |

- **Important:** Some viable IUPs have only 53% β-hCG rise in 2 days — avoid over-reliance on doubling time
- β-hCG in ectopic: can rise, fall, OR plateau — no single pattern is diagnostic
- 71% of ectopics: β-hCG rises more slowly than viable IUP and falls more slowly than spontaneous abortion

#### Discriminatory Zone
- Level of β-hCG above which IUP should be visible on TVUS
- Traditionally: 1,000–2,000 mIU/mL (TVUS); now some recommend 3,500 mIU/mL as more conservative
- β-hCG >2,000 without visible IUP = high suspicion for ectopic

#### Transvaginal Ultrasound (TVUS) — First-Line Imaging
- IUP visible at ~5–6 weeks gestation on TVUS
- Definitive diagnosis of ectopic: yolk sac and/or embryo visualized in adnexa (rare — most don't reach this stage)
- Findings suggesting ectopic:
  - Empty uterus (no intrauterine gestational sac)
  - Adnexal mass (ring of fire sign — vascular ring on color Doppler)
  - Free fluid in pelvis (hemoperitoneum)
  - Pseudogestational sac (decidual reaction within uterus — not a true sac)
- Interstitial ectopic: interstitial line sign, eccentrically located sac, myometrial mantle <5 mm
- 3D sonography: improves detection of interstitial implantation
- MRI: for inconclusive cases

#### Pregnancy of Unknown Location (PUL)
- Positive pregnancy test + no IUP or ectopic on ultrasound
- 50–70% of PULs are eventually ectopic or miscarriage
- 7–20% of PULs diagnosed as ectopic
- Requires serial β-hCG + serial ultrasound until location established

#### Additional Biomarkers (Emerging — Not Standard)
- Serum progesterone: <10 ng/mL suggests non-viable; >50 ng/mL suggests viable IUP; not specific
- Activin-AB: promising marker for ectopic
- PAPP-A: lower in ectopic than IUP
- ADAM-12, VEGF, miRNA (investigational)

#### Endometrial Sampling / D&C
- If β-hCG not rising normally and TVUS inconclusive
- D&C: if no chorionic villi → ectopic likely (highest specificity)
- Frozen section: reduces diagnostic timeline

### Management

#### Hemodynamically UNSTABLE (Ruptured Ectopic) — SURGICAL EMERGENCY
- Immediate surgical intervention (no time for extensive examination)
- Laparoscopic salpingectomy or salpingostomy
- Can convert to laparotomy if needed

#### Hemodynamically STABLE — Three Options:

**1. Surgical Management**
- **Salpingectomy:** removal of tube (preferred if contralateral tube normal)
- **Salpingostomy:** linear incision, remove ectopic, preserve tube (risk of persistent trophoblast)
- Laparoscopic approach preferred
- Indicated if:
  - Hemodynamic instability
  - Initial β-hCG level very high
  - Fetal cardiac activity detected outside uterus
  - Contraindication to methotrexate
  - Surgical emergency

**2. Medical Management — Methotrexate (MTX)**
- Intramuscular methotrexate (single-dose: 50 mg/m² or multi-dose protocol)
- Mechanism: folic acid antagonist → inhibits trophoblast cell division
- Eligibility criteria (single-dose):
  - Hemodynamically stable
  - No evidence of rupture
  - β-hCG typically <5,000 mIU/mL (some centers <10,000)
  - No fetal cardiac activity
  - No contraindications (renal/hepatic disease, bone marrow suppression, immunodeficiency, breastfeeding)
  - Ectopic <3.5 cm (some guidelines)
- Monitoring: β-hCG on days 4 and 7 post-treatment
- Success: β-hCG decreases >15% from day 4 to day 7
- Second dose needed in 15–20% of patients; >2 doses needed in ~1%
- Average time to β-hCG resolution: 33.6 days (can be up to 109 days)
- After MTX: β-hCG measured until <5 mIU/mL; avoid pregnancy for 3 months

**3. Expectant Management**
- For resolving ectopic (spontaneous regression/tubal abortion)
- Criteria: no evidence of rupture, clinically stable, asymptomatic, consistently declining β-hCG
- Most useful when initial β-hCG <1,000 IU/L
- Weekly (or more frequent) β-hCG + ultrasound monitoring
- Success rate: 47–82%

### Prognosis / Recurrence
- After one ectopic: 10% risk of subsequent ectopic
- After two ectopics: risk increases further
- Fertility generally preserved (especially after salpingostomy, though risk of persistent trophoblast)
- IVF recommended if recurrent ectopics or bilateral tube damage

## PART 2: EARLY PREGNANCY LOSS (MISCARRIAGE)

### Terminology (ACOG preferred — reVITALize definitions)
- **Miscarriage / intrauterine pregnancy loss** = spontaneous loss of nonviable intrauterine pregnancy before 20 weeks
- Preferred over "spontaneous abortion" (patient preference; reduces stigma)
- **Stillbirth / intrauterine fetal demise** = fetal death after 20 weeks

### Types of Miscarriage

| Type | Cervical Os | Products of Conception | Ultrasound |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Threatened miscarriage** | Closed | Not passed | Viable pregnancy present |
| **Inevitable miscarriage** | Open | Not yet passed | N/A |
| **Incomplete miscarriage** | Open | Partially passed | Heterogeneous tissue in cavity |
| **Complete miscarriage** | Closed | Fully passed | Empty uterus |
| **Missed miscarriage (missed abortion)** | Closed | Not passed | Non-viable pregnancy (no cardiac activity) |
| **Anembryonic pregnancy (blighted ovum)** | Closed | N/A | Gestational sac without embryo |
| **Recurrent miscarriage** | ≥3 consecutive losses | N/A | N/A |

### Incidence
- **10–15%** of clinically recognized pregnancies end in first-trimester loss
- Anembryonic pregnancy: may account for ~50% of all first-trimester miscarriages
- Age-related incidence:
  - Women 20–24 years: ~10% miscarriage rate
  - Women 40–44 years: ~51% miscarriage rate

### Etiology
- **Chromosomal / genetic abnormalities:** up to 50% of cases
- Uterine anomalies (septum, fibroids, Asherman syndrome)
- Thrombophilias (antiphospholipid syndrome — recurrent loss)
- Endocrine disorders (thyroid disease, uncontrolled diabetes, PCOS)
- Immunological factors
- Infection (listeria, toxoplasma, rubella, CMV)
- Sperm DNA fragmentation (role in recurrent loss)
- Obesity (associated with recurrent miscarriage)
- Subchorionic hematoma (threatened miscarriage → can progress to loss)
- Idiopathic (often unknown cause)

### Clinical Presentation
- Vaginal bleeding (can range from spotting to heavy)
- Lower abdominal cramping / pelvic pain
- Passage of tissue or clots
- Cessation of pregnancy symptoms (in missed miscarriage)

### Diagnosis

#### Ultrasound (Primary Tool)
- TVUS preferred over transabdominal
- IUP should be visible at ~5–6 weeks on TVUS
- **Diagnostic criteria for missed miscarriage (ACOG/RCOG/NICE):**
  - Crown-rump length ≥7 mm with no cardiac activity (RCOG/NICE: ≥7 mm)
  - Mean sac diameter ≥25 mm with no embryo (RCOG/NICE: ≥25 mm)
  - No embryo with heartbeat ≥2 weeks after scan showing gestational sac without yolk sac
  - No embryo with heartbeat ≥11 days after scan showing gestational sac with yolk sac
- **Anembryonic pregnancy (blighted ovum):**
  - Gestational sac without embryo
  - Mean sac diameter ≥25 mm (by TVUS)

#### β-hCG Serial Measurement
- Viable IUP: rises ≥49% over 48 hours (when initial <1,500 mIU/mL)
- Failing pregnancy (miscarriage): β-hCG decreasing ≥21% over 48 hours
- NICE guideline: <50% decrease OR <63% increase → refer for clinical review within 24 hours
- β-hCG plateaus around 10 weeks then naturally declines
- Discriminatory zone: 3,500 mIU/mL (recent recommendation) — IUP should be visible above this

#### Progesterone (Adjunct — NOT for PUL diagnosis per NICE)
- Low progesterone (<10 ng/mL) suggests non-viable pregnancy
- High progesterone (>50 ng/mL) suggests viable IUP
- Limited specificity; should not be used alone to diagnose ectopic vs. miscarriage

### Management

#### Threatened Miscarriage
- If cardiac activity confirmed: observation; most will continue normally
- No proven intervention stops miscarriage if destined to fail
- **Vaginal micronized progesterone 400 mg twice daily:** NICE recommends offering to women with confirmed IUP + vaginal bleeding + history of previous miscarriage (PROMISE trial data)
- Activity restriction not proven to help but commonly advised
- Avoid intercourse during active bleeding

#### Non-Viable Pregnancy — Three Options:

**1. Expectant Management**
- Wait for natural expulsion
- Suitable for: emotionally prepared, hemodynamically stable, no infection
- Time to complete: days to weeks (unpredictable)
- Success: ~50–80% within 2 weeks for incomplete; lower for missed miscarriage
- Follow-up TVUS to confirm complete

**2. Medical Management**
- **Misoprostol** (prostaglandin E1 analogue): vaginal, sublingual, or buccal administration
- Causes uterine contractions → expulsion of products of conception
- Dose: 800 mcg vaginally (most common for first-trimester loss)
- Success: ~80% complete expulsion within 2 weeks
- Side effects: cramping, bleeding, GI upset, fever
- Additional doses may be needed
- Follow-up TVUS to confirm complete

**3. Surgical Management**
- **Uterine aspiration (suction curettage / MVA / EVA)**
- D&C (dilatation and curettage)
- Preferred if:
  - Heavy bleeding
  - Signs of infection / septic miscarriage
  - Hemodynamic instability
  - Patient preference
  - Failed medical/expectant management
- Septic miscarriage: antibiotics + urgent surgical evacuation; infectious disease consult
- IUD can be placed immediately after surgical evacuation (NOT if intrauterine infection)

### Recurrent Pregnancy Loss (RPL)
- ≥2–3 consecutive pregnancy losses (definitions vary)
- Evaluation includes: karyotype (both partners), uterine anatomy (hysteroscopy/HSG), antiphospholipid antibodies, thyroid function, thrombophilia screen
- Antiphospholipid syndrome (APS): treat with low-dose aspirin + LMWH heparin during pregnancy

### Emotional Support
- Miscarriage is distressing; psychological well-being must be addressed
- Empathy, reassurance, grief counseling
- Nursing staff play key role in education, triage, emotional support
