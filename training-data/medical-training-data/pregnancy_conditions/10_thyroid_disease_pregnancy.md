## OVERVIEW
Thyroid disease is prevalent in pregnant women. Thyroid hormone is critical for fetal neurodevelopment — fetal thyroid matures at 18–20 weeks; before that, fetus depends entirely on maternal thyroid hormone via transplacental passage.

### Key Physiological Changes in Pregnancy
- Thyroid hormone production increases by **50%**
- Daily iodine requirement increases significantly
- Thyroid volume increases:
  - 10–20% in iodine-sufficient areas
  - Up to 40% in iodine-deficient areas
- hCG (especially in first trimester) has TSH-like activity → suppresses TSH → can cause transient low TSH / gestational thyrotoxicosis
- Increased thyroid-binding globulin (TBG) → increased total T4/T3 (use free T4/FT4 for assessment in pregnancy)
- Plasma iodide decreases (fetal use + increased renal clearance)
- ~15% of pregnant women: noticeable increase in thyroid gland size

### Prevalence of Thyroid Disorders in Pregnancy
| Condition | Prevalence |
|---|---|
| Overt hypothyroidism | ~0.5% |
| Subclinical hypothyroidism | ~3.5% |
| Total hypothyroidism | ~4% |
| Overt hyperthyroidism | ~0.6% |
| Subclinical hyperthyroidism | ~1.8% |
| Gestational transient thyrotoxicosis (GTT) | Up to 5% |

## PART 1: HYPOTHYROIDISM IN PREGNANCY

### Causes
- **Hashimoto thyroiditis** (most common in iodine-sufficient regions)
- Iodine deficiency (most common worldwide)
- Previous thyroid surgery or radioactive iodine treatment
- Medications (amiodarone, lithium, interferon)
- Central hypothyroidism (rare)

### Thyroid Antibodies
- **Thyroid peroxidase antibodies (TPOAb):** marker of Hashimoto thyroiditis
- TPOAb positive → increased adverse pregnancy outcomes independent of T3/T4 levels
- All pregnant women with TSH >2.5 mU/L should be assessed for TPOAb status (ATA)

### Effects on Fetal Development (Critical)
- Maternal thyroid hormone is sole source for fetus until mid-gestation
- Critical window: **8–14 weeks gestation** for thyroid hormone influence on fetal brain
- Untreated overt hypothyroidism: child IQ score average 7 points below mean; 3x increase in learning disabilities
- Neurodevelopmental outcomes affected: neuronal migration, myelination, synaptogenesis
- Both too low AND too high maternal FT4 = lower child IQ (U-shaped relationship)

### Adverse Pregnancy Outcomes of Untreated Overt Hypothyroidism
- Preeclampsia / gestational hypertension
- Gestational diabetes mellitus
- Placental abruption
- Preterm birth
- Cesarean section
- Infant ICU admission
- Miscarriage / pregnancy loss
- Low birth weight
- Fetal neurodevelopmental impairment

### TSH Reference Ranges in Pregnancy
- **Trimester-specific reference ranges** should be used (normal TSH lower in pregnancy due to hCG)
- General targets (when trimester-specific ranges unavailable):
  - First trimester: 0.1–2.5 mU/L
  - Second trimester: 0.2–3.0 mU/L
  - Third trimester: 0.3–3.0 mU/L
- TSH >4 mU/L in pregnancy: consider thyroid hormone replacement

### Treatment of Hypothyroidism in Pregnancy

#### Drug of Choice
- **Levothyroxine (T4)** — only T4 crosses placenta (NOT T3); T3 alone or combined T4/T3 cannot treat fetus
- Dose adjustments: titrate to keep TSH in trimester-specific targets

#### Pre-existing Hypothyroidism on Levothyroxine
- 50–85% of hypothyroid women need **30–50% dose increase** by 4–6 weeks gestation
- Monitor TSH every **4–6 weeks until week 20**, then at least once at week 30
- After delivery: return to pre-pregnancy dose
- Check TSH in each trimester

#### New Diagnosis of Hypothyroidism in Pregnancy
- Titrate rapidly to achieve TSH <2.6 mU/L
- TSH <3 mU/L acceptable in second/third trimesters

#### Subclinical Hypothyroidism (SCH) — Controversial
- Definition: TSH above upper reference limit + normal FT4
- ATA recommends treatment when:
  - TSH >10 mU/L (regardless of TPOAb)
  - TSH 2.5–10 mU/L + TPOAb positive
- Evidence: treatment generally beneficial for pregnancy outcomes, especially when initiated early

#### Screening
- Universal thyroid screening in pregnancy: NOT universally recommended (most guidelines)
- Targeted screening of high-risk patients: recommended by all guidelines
  - History of thyroid disease
  - Family history of thyroid disease
  - TPOAb positive
  - Goiter
  - Symptoms of thyroid disease
  - Previous pregnancy loss or preterm birth
  - Type 1 diabetes or other autoimmune disease
  - Prior head/neck radiation
  - Morbid obesity (BMI >40)
  - Older than 30

## PART 2: HYPERTHYROIDISM IN PREGNANCY

### Causes
| Cause | Frequency |
|---|---|
| Graves' disease (autoimmune) | 95% of pathological hyperthyroidism; 0.2% of all pregnancies |
| Gestational transient thyrotoxicosis (GTT) | Most common cause of low TSH in pregnancy (up to 5%) |
| Toxic multinodular goiter | Rare |
| Toxic adenoma | Rare |
| Subacute thyroiditis / postpartum thyroiditis | Rare in pregnancy |
| Struma ovarii | Very rare |
| Hydatidiform mole / choriocarcinoma | Rare |

### Types of Thyrotoxicosis in Pregnancy
1. **Gestational transient thyrotoxicosis (GTT)**
2. **Subclinical hyperthyroidism**
3. **Overt hyperthyroidism**

---

### A. Gestational Transient Thyrotoxicosis (GTT)
- Most common cause of low TSH in early pregnancy
- Due to hCG cross-reactivity with TSH receptors (hCG peak = 8–12 weeks)
- Characterized by: suppressed TSH ± elevated FT4, NO thyroid autoantibodies, no goiter, no exophthalmos
- Often associated with hyperemesis gravidarum (HG)
- Limited to first half of pregnancy (resolves spontaneously by ~18 weeks)
- **NO antithyroid drug (ATD) treatment required**
- Symptomatic treatment only (beta-blockers for tachycardia if needed, short-term)
- Key: distinguish from Graves' disease (measure TRAb)

### GTT vs. Graves' Disease — Differential Diagnosis
| Feature | GTT | Graves' Disease |
|---|---|---|
| TRAb (TSH receptor antibodies) | Negative | Positive (>95%) |
| Goiter | Absent | Often present |
| Exophthalmos/ophthalmopathy | Absent | May be present |
| Hyperemesis | Often present | Not typical |
| Onset | First trimester only | Any trimester |
| Natural course | Self-resolving by 18 wks | May worsen, then improve |
| Treatment | Supportive only | Antithyroid drugs |

---

### B. Graves' Disease in Pregnancy
- Autoimmune: TSH receptor antibodies (TRAb) stimulate thyroid → excess hormone production
- Occurs in 0.1–1% of pregnancies (0.4% overt)
- Natural course in pregnancy:
  - May worsen first trimester
  - Tends to **improve** in second/third trimester (immunotolerance)
  - ATD dose often needs to be decreased
  - Discontinuation of ATD possible in 20–30% of patients in last trimester

#### Adverse Effects of Untreated Graves' Disease
- Severe preeclampsia
- Maternal heart failure / congestive cardiac failure
- Thyroid storm (obstetric emergency)
- Preterm birth
- Miscarriage
- Stillbirth
- Low birth weight

#### Fetal/Neonatal Risks from Graves' Disease
- **Fetal hyperthyroidism:** TRAb crosses placenta → stimulates fetal thyroid
- **Neonatal hyperthyroidism:** TRAb persists in neonate after birth
- **Fetal hypothyroidism:** excessive ATD crosses placenta → fetal thyroid suppression
- **Neonatal hypothyroidism:** from ATD or maternal blocking TRAb
- Monitor TRAb at 18–22 weeks, and 30–34 weeks if still positive
- Fetal goiter on ultrasound: may indicate hypo- or hyperthyroidism in fetus

#### Treatment of Graves' Disease in Pregnancy

**Antithyroid Drugs (ATDs) — First-Line**
- Goal: maintain maternal FT4 at upper limit of normal reference range
- Keep ATD dose as LOW as possible (risk of fetal hypothyroidism)
- **Do NOT use block-replace regimen** in pregnancy (ATDs cross placenta more than levothyroxine → fetal hypothyroidism)

| Drug | Trimester | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **PTU (propylthiouracil)** | **First trimester preferred** | Less teratogenic than MMZ; 200–400 mg/day divided into 3 doses; greater side effects |
| **MMZ (methimazole/carbimazole)** | **Second + Third trimester preferred; also postpartum/breastfeeding** | 10–30 mg/day once daily; teratogenic in first trimester (choanal atresia, aplasia cutis) |

**Beta-Blockers**
- Propranolol: controls severe adrenergic symptoms (tachycardia, tremor, heat intolerance)
- Use SHORT-term (2–4 weeks); discontinue once symptoms improve

**Radioactive Iodine (I-131)**
- **ABSOLUTELY CONTRAINDICATED in pregnancy**
- Fetal thyroid begins concentrating iodide at ~12 weeks → risk of fetal hypothyroidism/ablation
- Women should not breastfeed for 4 months after I-131 treatment

**Thyroidectomy**
- Reserved for: significant non-compliance, severe adverse reactions to ATDs
- Optimal timing: **second trimester**
- Radioiodine: after delivery; radiation NOT during pregnancy

**Monitoring**
- TFTs every 4–6 weeks
- TRAb at 18–22 weeks; repeat at 30–34 weeks if elevated
- Fetal ultrasound for goiter if on ATDs + elevated TRAb

## PART 3: POSTPARTUM THYROIDITIS (PPT)
- Occurs in up to **10%** of all pregnancies
- Usually 6 weeks to 6 months after delivery (can occur up to 1 year later)
- Three phases possible:
  1. Hyperthyroid phase (weeks 1–4 post-delivery, lasting 1–2 months)
  2. Hypothyroid phase (months 4–8 post-delivery, lasting several months)
  3. Return to euthyroidism (most resolve within 1 year)
- **~20–40% progress to permanent hypothyroidism** → careful monitoring required
- Diagnosis: new-onset abnormal TSH + FT4 in postpartum period; TPOAb often positive
- Treatment:
  - Hyperthyroid phase: usually no ATD needed (not true Graves'); beta-blockers for symptoms
  - Hypothyroid phase: levothyroxine if symptomatic or severe
- Monitoring: annual TSH if history of PPT (high risk of permanent hypothyroidism)
- Important: women may be hypothyroid at time of subsequent pregnancy
