## PART 1: ANEMIA IN PREGNANCY

### Overview
- Most frequent hematologic complication during pregnancy
- ~40% of pregnant women worldwide affected
- Highest prevalence: South-East Asia (48.7%), Africa (46.3%)

### WHO Definition of Anemia in Pregnancy
| Trimester | Hemoglobin Threshold |
|---|---|
| First trimester | <11.0 g/dL (110 g/L) |
| Second trimester | <10.5 g/dL (105 g/L) |
| Third trimester | <11.0 g/dL (110 g/L) |

### Types of Anemia in Pregnancy

#### 1. Physiological (Dilutional) Anemia of Pregnancy
- Plasma volume increases 40–50%
- Red cell mass increases only 20–30%
- Net effect: fall in hemoglobin concentration
- Not pathological — does NOT require treatment
- Distinguish from pathological anemia (true Hb <11 g/dL)

#### 2. Iron Deficiency Anemia (IDA) — MOST COMMON CAUSE
- Accounts for majority of anemias diagnosed in pregnancy
- Daily iron requirement: increases from 18 mg/day (non-pregnant) → 27 mg/day (pregnant)
- Most women lack adequate iron stores at conception (chronic blood loss from menstruation)

**Diagnosis:**
- Serum ferritin: best marker of iron stores; <12–30 ng/mL = iron deficiency
- Microcytic, hypochromic anemia on CBC (low MCV, low MCH, low MCHC)
- Low serum iron, elevated TIBC
- Peripheral smear: microcytes, hypochromic red cells, anisocytosis, poikilocytosis

**Treatment:**
- Oral ferrous sulfate 325 mg (65 mg elemental iron) 1–3x/day
- Take with vitamin C to enhance absorption; avoid calcium, antacids, tea, coffee within 2 hours
- IV iron (ferric carboxymaltose, ferric gluconate): for those intolerant/non-responsive to oral iron; safe in 2nd and 3rd trimester
- Goal: replete stores before delivery

**Prevention:**
- Universal iron supplementation in pregnancy recommended (WHO): 30–60 mg elemental iron/day
- Shown to increase gestational duration and infant birth weight

**Complications of Severe Anemia (Hb <9 g/dL):**
- Preterm birth
- Low birth weight
- Increased maternal morbidity/mortality
- Poor fetal oxygenation
- Risk of blood transfusion at delivery

#### 3. Folate Deficiency Anemia
- Macrocytic anemia
- Increased demands in pregnancy
- Risk: neural tube defects in fetus if deficient periconceptionally
- Prevention: folic acid 400–800 mcg/day (high-risk women: 4–5 mg/day)
- Sources: leafy greens, legumes, fortified cereals

#### 4. Vitamin B12 Deficiency
- Macrocytic anemia + neurological symptoms
- Common in vegetarians/vegans
- Serum total B12 decreases in pregnancy (use holotranscobalamin — active fraction)
- Methylmalonic acid: most specific functional marker of B12 status
- Treatment: cyanocobalamin/hydroxocobalamin supplementation or injection

#### 5. Sickle Cell Disease (SCD) in Pregnancy
- Major risks: pain crises, acute chest syndrome, infections, preterm labor, IUGR, stillbirth
- Increased perinatal morbidity and mortality
- Management: folic acid 5 mg/day, hydroxyurea typically stopped in pregnancy, prophylactic transfusions considered, multidisciplinary care

#### 6. Thalassemia in Pregnancy
- Alpha and beta thalassemia trait: often asymptomatic; MCV low
- Major thalassemia: severe anemia; significant perinatal risk
- Screening: MCV <80 fL + normal ferritin → hemoglobin electrophoresis
- Partner testing important (carrier-carrier couple: 25% risk of affected fetus)

## PART 2: VENOUS THROMBOEMBOLISM (VTE) IN PREGNANCY

### Epidemiology
- VTE occurs in **1–2 per 1,000 pregnancies** (DVT + PE)
- **Leading cause of maternal mortality** in the United States
- PE accounts for ~20% of pregnancy-related deaths in the US
- Risk increase: 4–5x in antepartum period; **30–60x** in immediate postpartum period
- Daily absolute risk of VTE: **highest postpartum** (despite shorter duration than antepartum)

### DVT Distribution in Pregnancy
- 78–90% of pregnancy DVTs occur in the **left leg** (vs. 55% in non-pregnant)
- 72% in the **ilio-femoral vein** (vs. 9% in non-pregnant) — more likely to embolize
- ~2/3 of lower extremity DVTs occur antepartum (equally distributed across trimesters)
- PE: majority of pregnancy-related PE occurs **postpartum**; 64% after cesarean delivery

### Why Pregnancy Increases VTE Risk (Virchow's Triad)
1. **Hypercoagulability:**
   - Increased clotting factors (I, II, VII, VIII, X, XII)
   - Acquired resistance to activated Protein C
   - Decreased free Protein S
   - Impaired fibrinolysis
2. **Venous Stasis:**
   - Diminished venous flow to lower extremities
   - Compression of inferior vena cava and left iliac vein by gravid uterus
3. **Endothelial Damage:**
   - Occurs during vaginal delivery or cesarean section

### Pregnancy-Specific Risk Factors for VTE
- Preeclampsia
- Assisted reproductive techniques (IVF)
- Hemorrhagic complications
- Twin pregnancies / multiple gestation
- Cesarean delivery (especially emergency)
- Prolonged bed rest / immobility
- Obesity
- Prior VTE
- Thrombophilia (factor V Leiden, prothrombin gene mutation, antiphospholipid syndrome)
- Infection / inflammation

### Clinical Presentation

#### DVT
- Unilateral leg pain and swelling (most common)
- May include erythema, warmth, tenderness
- Left leg much more commonly affected
- Homans' sign (pain with foot dorsiflexion): NOT reliable; not sensitive or specific

#### Pulmonary Embolism (PE)
- Dyspnea / shortness of breath (most common — 34.7%)
- Tachycardia (30.4%)
- Chest pain (13%)
- Leg pain or weakness (9.6%)
- Hypoxia (low oxygen saturation)
- Pleuritic chest pain, hemoptysis (massive/submassive PE)
- Hemodynamic instability (massive PE)
- Note: many PE symptoms overlap with normal pregnancy symptoms

### Diagnosis

#### D-Dimer
- Elevated in normal pregnancy → **standard cutoffs NOT valid in pregnancy**
- Rising throughout pregnancy; cannot be used to rule out VTE with standard thresholds
- Adjusted (pregnancy-adapted) YEARS algorithm uses higher D-dimer threshold

#### DVT Diagnosis
1. **Compression duplex ultrasonography (CUS):** First-line test for DVT
   - If proximal DVT confirmed → treat (same as PE)
   - If negative but iliac vein thrombosis suspected → MR venography or contrast venography
   - Clinical diagnosis confirmed in only ~10% of pregnant women (vs. 25% non-pregnant)
2. If CUS inconclusive: repeat in 1 week or escalate imaging

#### PE Diagnosis
1. **Clinical assessment + D-dimer** (pregnancy-adapted YEARS algorithm)
   - YEARS criteria: clinical DVT signs, hemoptysis, PE most likely diagnosis
   - No YEARS criteria + D-dimer <1000 ng/mL → PE excluded
   - ≥1 YEARS criteria + D-dimer <500 ng/mL → PE excluded
   - Shown to avoid CTPA in 39% of suspected PE cases in pregnancy

2. **Bilateral lower limb CUS:** Do first if DVT suspected (avoids radiation)

3. **CT Pulmonary Angiography (CTPA) — Test of Choice for PE**
   - Fetal radiation exposure: <130 μGy (less than V/Q scan)
   - Concern: maternal breast radiation exposure (higher than V/Q)
   - Most accurate; can detect subsegmental PE

4. **V/Q (Ventilation-Perfusion) Scan**
   - Fetal radiation: ~370 μGy (higher than CTPA)
   - Less breast radiation than CTPA
   - Preferred if normal chest X-ray and clinical suspicion moderate

5. **Chest X-ray:** Often normal; can help guide imaging choice

6. **Echo:** May show right heart strain in massive PE

### Treatment

#### Anticoagulation — Cornerstone of Treatment

**LMWH (Low Molecular Weight Heparin) — Preferred in Pregnancy**
- Does NOT cross placenta
- Predictable pharmacokinetics
- Safe for mother and fetus
- Options: enoxaparin, dalteparin, tinzaparin
- Dose: therapeutic (weight-based, twice daily)
- Duration: minimum **3 months total** from diagnosis; **continue until 6 weeks postpartum**
- Monitor anti-Xa levels in very high/low body weight, renal impairment

**Unfractionated Heparin (UFH)**
- Does not cross placenta; safe
- Used in specific situations: renal failure, near delivery (easier reversal), unstable PE
- Requires aPTT monitoring
- Subcutaneous UFH or continuous IV infusion

**Warfarin**
- **Contraindicated during pregnancy** (teratogenic; causes fetal hemorrhage)
- Safe postpartum and during breastfeeding
- Can switch to warfarin postpartum (with LMWH bridge until INR therapeutic)

**Direct Oral Anticoagulants (DOACs) — apixaban, rivaroxaban, dabigatran**
- **Avoid in pregnancy** (cross placenta; limited safety data; fetal risk likely)
- Can use postpartum/breastfeeding (rivaroxaban preferred — less excretion in breast milk per emerging data; consult specialist)

#### Peripartum Management
- Stop LMWH 24 hours before planned delivery (elective CS or induction)
- Stop LMWH 12–24 hours before epidural/spinal anesthesia
- Neuraxial anesthesia contraindicated within 12–24 hours of therapeutic LMWH
- Restart anticoagulation 4–6 hours after vaginal delivery, 6–12 hours after CS

#### Massive PE (Hemodynamically Unstable)
- Emergency stabilization (ICU)
- Consider: thrombolysis (alteplase), catheter-directed thrombus fragmentation, surgical embolectomy
- Significant fetal morbidity/mortality risk with invasive procedures
- Reserve for: failed anticoagulation, massive PE, acute limb-threatening DVT

#### IVC Filter
- Reserved for: contraindication to anticoagulation + documented VTE
- High complication rate; temporary filters preferred

### Prophylaxis
- Women with prior VTE or high-risk thrombophilia → prophylactic LMWH throughout pregnancy + 6 weeks postpartum
- Antiphospholipid syndrome → prophylactic or therapeutic LMWH + low-dose aspirin
- Risk assessment at booking and at each clinical encounter

### Postpartum Complications
- Postthrombotic syndrome (PTS): almost half of DVT patients develop reduced quality of life
- Compression stockings: reduce PTS risk
- VTE risk remains elevated 6–12 weeks postpartum
